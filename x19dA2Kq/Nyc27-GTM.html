The engine · Commercial Director's operating notes

How sales and marketing work — and how everyone connects

Our GTM motion is event-led with a sales-led overlay: marketing builds the audience and the demand, sales converts named accounts, and the event itself is the conversion machine — it compresses months of B2B funnel into three days. That only works if the handoffs between teams are defined, fast and measured. This section is the definition.

How marketing and sales divide the work

Two engines, one funnel

Marketing creates and captures demand: the density narrative and speaker proof create future buyers (brand/demand, ~60% of effort); the waitlist, applications and operator programme capture buyers who already know (lead gen, ~40%). Sales converts named accounts: renewals first, then tier-led outbound into a finite, known market — this is a concentrated industry where the buyer list is knowable, which is exactly where named-account selling beats volume prospecting.

The handoff · the highest-failure point in B2B

Defined once, measured weekly

Lead: any captured contact (waitlist, application, download). MQL: right buyer type + seniority from a target segment, engaged. SQL: sponsor-intent — enquiry, stage ask, stand ask, or an MQL from a named account. Every handoff to sales carries source, fit, context and requested next action. Sales accepts or rejects every SQL within 24 hours — silence counts as a miss for both sides.

Attribution · decided once

First touch, never litigated

The source field is set at first capture and never overwritten; deals credit the first touch. One model, applied all cycle, reviewed once in the post-event readout. Attribution arguments consume more energy than they return — we spend that energy on the funnel's worst leak instead, found stage-by-stage in the weekly numbers.

Strategy · pipeline discipline

Coverage before closing

  • 3x coverage rule: open pipeline must cover the next quarter's locked targets three times over — the enterprise benchmark, and our leading indicator in the quiet autumn months. December's €268k needs ~€800k in play by October; February's €334k needs ~€1M by November.
  • Velocity levers: revenue = deals × deal size × win rate ÷ cycle length. Each phase pulls one lever: rebooks shorten the cycle, tier-led anchoring raises deal size, proof (speakers, ticket momentum) raises win rate, the spikes add deal count.
  • Weighted forecast: stage-weighted pipeline reviewed weekly against the locked month — gap named per account, never per vibe.

Strategy · the funnel

Create, capture, convert

  • Create demand (~60% of effort): the density narrative, category authority and speaker proof — brand work that makes future buyers. This is why the speaker engine runs weekly even when this week's tickets don't need it.
  • Capture demand (~40%): waitlist, speaker applications, operator passes, retargeting pools — mechanisms that catch buyers who already know.
  • Convert: map content to stage — awareness gets the density story and speaker reveals; consideration gets the agenda, FAQ and tier comparisons; decision gets price-gap urgency, logistics and group offers. One asset cannot do all three jobs.
Strategy · campaign physics

Respect how people buy

  • Buying behaviour: roughly a third of event tickets sell a month or more out; about a quarter sell in the final weeks. The Late arc is not a failure mode — it is physics. Plan creative, support and capacity for the final-fortnight surge while Early Bird does the margin work.
  • Launch arc: tease (waitlist warm-up) → launch burst (weeks 1–3 carry the campaign's heaviest spend) → sustain → deadline spike at every phase switch. Deadlines are our strongest converter; that is why phases never extend.
  • Fix leaks before adding volume: when pacing slips, find the worst-converting funnel step (page → start → purchase) and fix that — more top-of-funnel spend on a leaking funnel buys nothing.
Strategy · the three numbers

Leading, lagging, counter

  • Leading: waitlist growth, purchase starts, CPL, sponsor enquiries passed to sales — these steer the week.
  • Lagging: paid tickets vs the date targets (150 / 350 / 700 / 1,150) and net revenue vs €750k–€1.2M.
  • Counter: buyer quality — the executive mix of actual purchasers — and refund rate. We never buy cheap volume that dilutes the room we sell sponsors.
