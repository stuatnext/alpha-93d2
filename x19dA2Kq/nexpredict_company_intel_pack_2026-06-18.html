Operating thesis

Prediction markets are no longer one market. They are several industries trying to define the same product at once.

Finance sees event contracts and market structure. Sportsbooks see substitution risk and optionality. Compliance teams see employee conduct and MNPI exposure. Media sees a new probability layer and a newsroom ethics headache. Data companies see settlement and integrity infrastructure. Crypto sees embedded distribution. Regulators see a category that refuses to sit politely in one box.

The blunt read: demand is no longer the interesting question. Trust architecture is.

What to do now
  1. Use the top 12 P1 sponsor leads this week, not next month.
  2. Lock agenda tracks around compliance, official data, institutional execution and World Cup perception.
  3. Relationship team should invite tier-one CEOs as category shapers, not as ticket prospects.
  4. Marketing should stop selling a hot topic and sell the room where the definition gets fought over.
  5. Editorial should use the intel to create a recurring market-structure beat.

Best sales line

The operators are no longer theorising. They are filing, partnering, launching and building. If prediction markets are even close to your commercial roadmap, NEXTPredict is where the category is being defined.

Best marketing line

The category is moving faster than the language around it. NEXTPredict is the room for people who need to understand the operating system before it hardens around bad assumptions.

Best editorial line

Prediction markets are scaling through sport, institutionalising through infrastructure, and being stress-tested by every unresolved question around trust.

Department action map

Conference content

Priority: turn the intel into agenda architecture. The conference must not look like a platform demo day. It should feel like a market structure summit with serious commercial tension.

  • Build the 10 tracks below.
  • Secure anchor voices from TT, StarCompliance, Sportradar, Wedbush, TS Imagine, Novig and Bloomberg before long-tail founders.
  • Create one CFTC/state-federal session with legal balance.
  • Use the World Cup as the audience hook, then broaden into trust, settlement, institutional workflows and consumer protection.

Sales and sponsorship

Priority: sell lanes, not logos. The money is in infrastructure, compliance, data, legal and institutional distribution.

  • Use the P1 sponsor table as the first ABM sprint.
  • Lead with why the company now has a category stake.
  • Offer track ownership, private roundtables, research briefings and executive meetings.
  • Avoid generic bronze/silver/gold outreach for P1 accounts.

Relationship team

Priority: tier-one people should receive category invitations, not transactional ticket copy.

  • Frame the invitation as shaping the definition of prediction markets.
  • Use private dinners, advisory conversations and moderated interviews.
  • Keep Kalshi and Polymarket language neutral: category leaders, market formation, liquidity and infrastructure.
  • Do not pitch anti-sportsbook or anti-gaming language to operator leaders.

Ticket sales and marketing

Priority: build campaigns by audience pain, not by event features.

  • Finance: access, clearing, risk, market quality.
  • Compliance: employee conduct, MNPI, surveillance.
  • Sports: World Cup, data, settlement, public perception.
  • Media/comms: brand exposure, newsroom ethics, narrative risk.
  • Crypto/distribution: wallets, exchanges, white-label rails.

Lead generation

Priority: each signal becomes a HubSpot segment.

  • Tag by lane: infrastructure, compliance, sports-data, legal, crypto-distribution, media-risk, institutional-capital.
  • Create account notes from the personalised intel.
  • Sales should reference the signal, not explain prediction markets from scratch.
  • Every editorial feature should produce a follow-up list for sales.

Editorial

Priority: own the B2B market-structure beat.

  • Write fewer generic growth pieces.
  • Prioritise specific signals: TT, Wedbush, StarCompliance, Hadrius, Sportradar, CFTC, ProPublica, Bloomberg, Yale, NYMag.
  • Build named-source opportunities for tagging and follow-up.
  • Always soften unverified numbers.
