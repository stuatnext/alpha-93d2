Company operating model

Project Commercialisation Operating Model

A standard route for moving projects from evidence to commercial model, market strategy, execution setup, launch and review.

Evergreen model Applies to events and media Owner: Commercial Use with product modules

Purpose

This model gives the company one operating route for commercialising projects. It defines the sequence, ownership, required inputs, approval checkpoints and escalation rules.

It is designed to stop teams scaling sales, marketing, CRM, collateral and delivery activity before the commercial foundation is stable.

What this is not

  • It is not a GTM playbook.
  • It is not a marketing calendar.
  • It is not a project delivery plan.
  • It is not a replacement for event, media, podcast or news modules.
  • It is not a brainstorming document. Ideas should be gathered before approval meetings and presented as clear recommendations.

1. Scope

This operating model applies to all commercial projects, including events, media products, podcasts, news products, newsletters, video series, sponsored campaigns, commercial launches and new product ideas.

Project type changes the depth and timing of the process. It does not change the order of decisions.

Large, high risk or high revenue projects need the most detail. Smaller projects need less detail, but they should still follow the same basic sequence.

2. Core logic

0. Confirm project type

Agree what kind of project it is and how much planning it needs.

1. Evidence

Collect inputs before strategy work starts.

2. Commercial model

Build thesis, pricing, packages, revenue pacing and financial logic.

3. Market strategy

Turn the commercial model into GTM direction and functional KPIs.

4. Execution setup

Prepare sales, marketing, CRM, collateral and workflows.

5. Launch and review

Execute, optimise, report and feed the next cycle.

3. Non negotiables

Evidence before strategy

Commercial recommendations need evidence from sales, delivery, partnerships, finance, audience, content, media, marketing, competitors and clients.

Strategy before scale

Sales, marketing, CRM and collateral should not scale until the commercial model is stable.

Decisions before assets

Final sales and campaign assets require approved products, pricing, packages or clear guardrails.

  • Sales and marketing execute the approved direction. They do not reopen commercial strategy.
  • CRM requirements start during commercial model development.
  • Board level financial targets should be known before the commercial model is approved. This includes EBITA, margin, cash, revenue ambition and acceptable investment risk.
  • Monthly partnerships sales targets and monthly ticket sales targets are set during Stage 2 once products, pricing, packages and major strategy decisions are clear.
  • Missing inputs become named risks.
  • Late inputs do not reopen approved strategy by default.
  • Every approval has an owner, deadline and escalation route.
  • Every blocker has an owner, deadline and consequence.
  • Fast track routes require approval from Stuart, Ana or Pierre.

5. Board and financial target inputs

Commercial decisions need board level financial context. Revenue alone is not enough. A project can hit revenue and still damage profit, cash or delivery capacity.

Before the commercial model is approved, Commercial should understand the financial health targets leadership will use to judge the project.
Financial input Why Commercial needs it Owner Timing
EBITA target Shows the profit expectation behind the project, not only the sales ambition. Executive Sponsor, Commercial Approver, Finance Stage 1, before commercial approval
Revenue ambition Gives Commercial a starting point for the size of the opportunity. The final revenue target is recommended after products, pricing, packages and major strategy choices are clear. Commercial Lead, Commercial Approver Early ambition in Stage 1. Final recommendation in Stage 2.
Monthly partnerships sales target Shows how much partnership revenue needs to be closed each month to hit the approved commercial model. Commercial Lead with Sales Lead input Stage 2
Monthly ticket sales target Shows how many paid tickets and how much ticket revenue need to be delivered each month. Commercial Lead with Sales Lead, Marketing Lead and Finance input Stage 2
Gross margin or contribution margin target Prevents product and package decisions that look strong on revenue but weak on profit. Finance, Commercial Lead Stage 1
Expected costs Shows the likely spend, major cost lines and cost risks so Commercial can protect profit. Finance, Project Owner, Commercial Lead Stage 1
Cash and payment expectations Clarifies payment timing, invoicing risk, refund exposure and cash flow pressure. Finance Stage 1
Discount tolerance Sets the boundary for discounting before Sales starts creating margin leakage. Commercial Lead, Commercial Approver, Finance Stage 2
Strategic investment tolerance Clarifies whether leadership accepts lower short term profit for strategic growth, category entry, venue change, audience repositioning or market positioning. Executive Sponsor, Commercial Approver Stage 1 or Stage 2
Financial red lines Defines what cannot be breached, such as minimum margin, maximum spend, unacceptable cash exposure or non-negotiable EBITA contribution. Executive Sponsor, Commercial Approver, Finance Before commercial approval

If these targets are not available, Commercial should proceed with named assumptions and flag the decision risk. Leadership should not later judge the strategy against financial targets that were not provided during the model build.

7. Stage detail

Stage 0: Confirm project type and planning level

1 to 2 working days

Purpose: Make the project easy to plan from the start. This stage confirms what kind of project it is, how commercially important it is, how much detail the planning needs and who needs to approve decisions.

Inputs required

  • Project type
  • Revenue ambition
  • EBITA target or expected profit contribution
  • Margin expectation
  • Expected costs
  • Cash or investment sensitivity
  • Strategic importance
  • Commercial risk
  • Delivery complexity
  • Timeline pressure

Outputs

  • Project type confirmed
  • Planning level agreed
  • Approval owner named
  • Required input list
  • Expected timeline
  • Fast track eligibility
Project type Planning level Typical approval level
Flagship event, major launch, high value media product Detailed planning: full evidence pack, full commercial model, full approval process and full launch readiness check. Commercial Lead, Executive Sponsor and Commercial Approver
Regional event, existing media product, podcast season, strategic campaign Standard planning: core evidence pack, commercial model, approval from relevant owners and standard launch readiness check. Commercial Lead with relevant functional approval
Small campaign, tactical sponsorship, minor product update Simplified planning: short evidence pack, clear commercial assumption, named approver and basic launch readiness check. Commercial Lead or delegated owner

Stage 1: Evidence and inputs

5 to 10 working days

Purpose: Gather the evidence Commercial needs before strategy work starts.

Inputs required

  • Sales performance, churn, objections, pricing resistance, discounts and sales team insight
  • Client, sponsor, advertiser or partner feedback, fulfilment issues and handoff issues
  • Operational constraints, capacity and critical path
  • Expected costs, EBITA expectations, margin assumptions, fees, tax and payment issues
  • Content, audience, media, marketing and website performance
  • Competitor intel, market movement and buyer behaviour changes
  • Known risks, dependencies and missing data

Outputs

  • Completed input pack
  • Missing input list
  • Known delivery risks
  • Baseline assumptions
  • Initial RAG status
  • Commercial readiness decision

Input completion rule

If required inputs are incomplete after the agreed window, Commercial proceeds using named assumptions. Missing inputs become owned risks. Late inputs do not reopen approved strategy unless Stuart, Ana or Pierre confirm material impact.

Input status Action
Complete Move into commercial model build.
Partial Proceed with stated assumptions and named risk owner.
Missing Proceed unless the missing input blocks pricing, margin, delivery viability or contractual risk.
Critical blocker Escalate within 24 hours. Options are to approve an assumption, delay only the affected decision, or assign a final deadline.

Stage 2: Commercial model

1 to 3 weeks

Purpose: Build the commercial foundation before GTM, collateral, CRM and execution scale.

Work covered

  • Product, format or inventory review
  • Event product discussion with Conference Content Lead where agenda, speakers, content quality or sponsor integration affects commercial value
  • Pricing review
  • Package structure
  • Revenue model and monthly pacing
  • Monthly partnerships sales targets
  • Monthly ticket sales targets
  • EBITA and financial health check
  • Margin check
  • Discount rules
  • Rebooking or renewal logic
  • Structural revenue leak review
  • CRM requirements draft

Outputs

  • Commercial thesis
  • Product, format or package recommendation
  • Pricing and discount rules
  • Recommended revenue target and assumptions after products, pricing, packages and major strategy decisions are clear
  • Monthly partnerships sales target
  • Monthly ticket sales target
  • EBITA, margin, cash and expected cost assumptions
  • Inventory model
  • Risk register
  • CRM requirements draft
  • Decision log update

Stage 3: Market strategy

1 to 2 weeks

Purpose: Translate the approved commercial model into market direction for Sales, Marketing, CRM, Project Owners, Partnerships, Content and Media.

Inputs required

  • Approved or near approved commercial model
  • Approved financial targets or assumptions
  • Approved pricing or guardrails
  • Monthly partnerships sales and ticket sales targets from Stage 2
  • Sales account insight, client feedback and competitor intel
  • Marketing and audience insight
  • Operational constraints
  • Sponsor, advertiser or partner handoff requirements
  • CRM reporting requirements draft

Outputs

  • GTM framework
  • Positioning
  • Buyer and audience priorities
  • Functional KPI direction for Sales Lead and Marketing Lead
  • Sales direction
  • Marketing direction
  • Rebooking or renewal approach
  • Handover points
  • Reporting requirements
The GTM should explain how the approved commercial model goes to market. It should not reopen the product, pricing or revenue model unless a material issue is found.

Stage 4: Execution setup

1 to 3 weeks

Purpose: Turn strategy into functional plans, workflows, assets and reporting before market activity scales.

This stage runs as parallel workstreams. CRM should not sit behind Sales and Marketing as late admin.

Workstream Owner Output
Sales strategy Sales Lead Sales plan, account priorities, renewal or rebooking plan, NRR target, price increase target, monthly sales pacing and discount boundaries.
Marketing strategy Marketing Lead Campaign plan, channel plan, website requirements, creative direction, launch calendar, lead targets, audience quality targets and conversion targets.
CRM setup CRM Lead Fields, forms, source tracking, dashboards, workflows, handover stages and reporting cadence.
Collateral and enablement Commercial, Sales and Marketing Collateral brief, deck structure, rate card, one pagers, proposal templates and objection handling.
Delivery workflow Project Owner and Partnerships Lead Handover process, fulfilment requirements, proof capture and owner map.
Reporting setup Commercial and CRM Lead Weekly report structure, dashboard views, source reporting and risk tracking.

Stage 5: Launch, optimisation and review

Project specific

Purpose: Move into market activity, track performance and feed learning into the next cycle.

Inputs required

  • Approved pricing or guardrails
  • Sales and marketing plans
  • Sales team and marketing team KPIs
  • CRM setup
  • Collateral and landing pages
  • Tracking links and source rules
  • Delivery handoff process

Outputs

  • Sales launch
  • Campaign launch
  • Weekly reporting
  • Sponsor, advertiser or buyer feedback
  • Renewal or rebooking pipeline
  • Performance review
  • Next cycle input pack
The model does not end at launch. Performance review creates the evidence base for the next commercial cycle.

KPIs should follow the commercial model. They should not be invented separately by each function.

KPI level Who sets it When it is set Examples

Sales Lead KPIs Commercial Lead Stage 3, once the GTM direction is clear Partnerships revenue, monthly close targets, rebooking target, upsell target, pipeline coverage, weighted pipeline, discount discipline, strategic account progress.
Marketing Lead KPIs Commercial Lead Stage 3, once the GTM direction is clear Ticket demand contribution, lead targets, source quality, conversion targets, audience quality, campaign reporting, website conversion, paid efficiency.
Sales team KPIs Sales Lead Stage 4, when the sales strategy is built Rep targets, account ownership, activity targets, proposal targets, close targets, renewal targets, pipeline hygiene.
Marketing team KPIs Marketing Lead Stage 4, when the marketing strategy is built Campaign output, channel targets, creative testing, lead volume, buyer quality, conversion support, reporting cadence.
CRM and reporting KPIs Commercial Lead and CRM Lead Stage 4, before activity scales Data completeness, source tracking, dashboard accuracy, handover compliance, reporting cadence.

9. Approval checkpoints

Approval checkpoint Decision owner Decision required Output
Checkpoint A: Input readiness Commercial Lead Are inputs complete enough to build strategy, or should Commercial proceed with assumptions? Proceed, proceed with assumptions, pause affected decision or escalate.
Checkpoint B: Commercial approval Commercial Approver with Commercial Lead Are product, pricing, package, revenue, EBITA, margin, cash, monthly sales pacing and risk assumptions approved? Approved commercial foundation or required changes.
Checkpoint C: Execution dependency approval Project Owner and Partnerships Lead Are delivery dependencies, handoffs and fulfilment requirements clear? Approved execution dependencies and named blockers.
Checkpoint D: Launch readiness Functional leads Are sales, marketing, CRM, collateral, team KPIs and reporting ready to scale? Launch approval, conditional launch or blocker escalation.

Allowed decision outcomes

  • Approved: Move to the next stage.
  • Approved with conditions: Move forward with named conditions, owner and deadline.
  • Rejected with required changes: Do not move forward until the required change is made.

10. Escalation logic

Approval window rule

Approval owners have 5 working days to approve, approve with conditions or reject with required changes. If no decision is received, Stuart escalates to Ana or Pierre.

Issue Default action Escalates to
Low risk decision delayed Proceed with documented assumption. Commercial Lead
Pricing, EBITA, margin, cash, monthly sales target or revenue decision delayed Escalate. Do not treat silence as approval. Commercial Approver
Cross team conflict Escalate with options and recommendation. Executive Sponsor
Delivery or sponsor fulfilment risk Escalate with operational impact and commercial recommendation. Project Owner, Partnerships Lead, Executive Sponsor if unresolved
Fast track route requested Record assumption, risk accepted, decision owner and next checkpoint. Stuart, Ana or Pierre

11. Fast track rule

Stuart, Ana or Pierre may approve a compressed route when speed matters.

Every compressed route must record:

  • The reason speed matters.
  • The assumption being made.
  • The risk accepted.
  • The decision owner.
  • The next checkpoint.
Fast track does not mean no governance. It means fewer steps, clearer risk ownership and faster decisions.

12. RAG status: Red, Amber, Green

RAG means Red, Amber, Green. It gives Ana and the leadership team a quick view of whether a project is ready, at risk or blocked.

Status Meaning Action
Green Inputs complete, commercial foundation approved, market strategy ready, execution setup on track. Proceed.
Amber Inputs partial, commercial foundation close, blockers named and owned. Proceed with assumptions or conditional approval.
Red Missing inputs, unapproved pricing, missing EBITA or margin targets, unclear ownership or unresolved delivery risk. Escalate and resolve the affected decision.
Blue Fast track route approved. Track risk, owner and next checkpoint.

13. Decision log

Every priority project should maintain a decision log. The Commercial Lead owns it. The Project Owner keeps it updated where decisions relate to delivery, dependencies or project timing. This prevents strategy being reopened by memory, opinion or late feedback.

14. Change control

After commercial approval, product, format, pricing and package changes require a change request.

Change request field Required detail
Requested change What needs to change.
Commercial reason Why the change matters.
Impact Effect on revenue, EBITA, margin, cash, delivery, Sales, Marketing, CRM and collateral.
Approval required Commercial Lead and Commercial Approver for major changes.
Decision Approved, approved with conditions or rejected.

15. Meeting rule

  • Pre read required.
  • Brainstorming should happen asynchronously before the meeting.
  • Ideas should be submitted as recommendations with evidence, impact and owner where possible.
  • Meetings approve, reject or assign.
  • No open brainstorming in approval meetings.
  • Every blocker gets an owner and deadline.
  • Every meeting ends with a decision log update.
